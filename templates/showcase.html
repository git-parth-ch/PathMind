A* combines actual cost g(n) (distance traveled) and heuristic h(n) (Manhattan distance to goal) into f(n) = g + h. This makes it smarter than UCS — it prioritizes nodes closer to the goal. GPS systems, game NPCs, and robotics all use A* for its speed and optimality guarantee.

🚨 WHY IT'S WRONG: Because Greedy only tracks h (visible raw distance to the goal) without remembering how far it just walked, it blindly ignores the walls enclosing it. It mathematically traps itself at the red marker because every available step away from the wall forces h to increase, causing it to backtrack endlessly in a local minimum! A* safely avoids this by calculating total journey cost.

Greedy uses only h(n) — the straight-line guess to the goal. It's fast but not optimal. In tricky mazes it charges toward the goal and gets stuck, exploring many dead ends. A* avoids this by also tracking actual cost. Early GPS units used greedy-style heuristics — they often gave bad routes!

Bidirectional BFS runs two simultaneous searches — one from the source forward, one from the destination backward. They meet in the middle. This can reduce nodes explored from b^d to 2×b^(d/2) — exponentially fewer! Network routing protocols like OSPF use this principle to find paths faster in large graphs.
